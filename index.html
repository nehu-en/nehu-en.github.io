DENSITY

Density is the amount of mass an object has in relation to its volume, and it is typically expressed in kilograms per cubic meter (kg/mÂ³). In the case of an asteroid impacting Earth, density is a key factor because:
* It determines the type of material the asteroid is made of (rocky, metallic, or icy).
* It affects its total mass, even if it is the same size as another, less dense asteroid.
* It influences the impact energy, since a higher density means more mass and therefore a more powerful collision.
In impact calculations, density is used together with size, velocity, and entry angle to estimate the released energy, the resulting crater, and the global effects of the impact.

DIAMETER

The diameter is the measurement of the total width of a spherical object, passing through its center. In calculations involving an asteroid falling to Earth, the diameter is used to:
* Determine the size of the asteroid.
* Calculate its volume (and, together with density, its total mass).
* Estimate the impact energy, since a larger asteroid releases much more energy upon collision.
In summary, the diameter directly influences the destructive force of the impact and the size of the crater it produces.

ANGLE OF IMPACT

The impact angle is the angle at which an asteroid enters the atmosphere and strikes the Earth's surface, measured relative to the ground. In impact calculations, it is used to:
* Determine the shape and size of the crater (low angles produce more elongated craters).
* Estimate how much energy is transferred to the ground, since more vertical impacts are more destructive.
* Assess atmospheric effects, such as the dispersion of fragments or the shock wave.
The impact angle influences the intensity and distribution of the damage caused by the asteroid.

IMPACT SPEED

Impact velocity is the speed at which the asteroid collides with Earth, typically measured in kilometers per second (km/s). In impact calculations, it is used to:
* Determine the energy released in the collision (higher velocity means greater energy).
* Calculate the temperature and pressure generated during the impact.
* Estimate the effects of the collision, such as the crater size, shock wave, and global damage.
In summary, impact velocity defines the destructive power of the asteroid upon striking Earth.

MITIGATION

Early detection

Use telescopes and satellites to discover and track near-Earth asteroids. The earlier an asteroid is detected, the easier it is to divert.

Deflection methods (if there are decades of lead time)

Kinetic impactor: a spacecraft collides with the asteroid to nudge it slightly. Gravity tractor: a nearby spacecraft slowly attracts the asteroid using its gravity. Attached thrusters: engines installed on the asteroid to alter its trajectory. Controlled Yarkovsky effect:
modify how the asteroid reflects or emits heat so that its orbit changes over time.

Fragmentation methods (if time is short)

Nearby nuclear explosion: heats and ejects material from the asteroid, altering its trajectory. Conventional explosives: less powerful, only effective for small objects.

Contingency plans (if the impact is unavoidable)

Evacuation of the impact zone. International coordination (UN, NASA, ESA) to minimize human and material damage.

WHEN THE ASTEROID HITS THE EARTH

Consequences if the Impact is on Mainland

Massive crater and ejection: Formation of a hole several kilometers in diameter, ejecting rocks that cause secondary global impacts. Immediate local destruction: Vaporization of everything within the radius, with shock waves that topple buildings hundreds of kilometers away. Fires and terrestrial dust: Increased fires from dry vegetation, and dust clouds that locally darken the sky. Earthquakes and landslides: Induced tremors causing avalanches and subsidence in nearby mountains. Soil contamination: Release of heavy metals and radiation if the asteroid is metallic. Lower tsunami risk: Only local flooding if rivers or lakes are affected, but greater damage to densely populated continental areas.

Consequences if the Impact is on Water (Ocean)

Giant tsunamis: Waves hundreds of meters high that flood coastlines thousands of kilometers away, causing more deaths than a land impact. Water vaporization: Steam explosions that deplete the ozone layer for years, increasing global UV radiation. Vapor clouds and rainfall: Climate disruptions with extreme storms and accelerated ocean acidification. Damage to marine life: Mass deaths of fish, corals, and plankton due to shock waves and contaminants. Smaller visible crater: Water cushions the impact but may trigger volcanic eruptions if tectonic plates are affected. Amplified coastal impacts: Higher risk for port cities, with soil salinization in agricultural areas and freshwater contamination.

METEORS IMPACTED AND NEARBY

Chelyabinsk (Rusia, 2013)

The Chelyabinsk event occurred on February 15, 2013, in Russia, when a meteor approximately 20 meters in diameter entered the Earth's atmosphere and exploded at an altitude of about 30 km. The shockwave damaged thousands of buildings and caused minor injuries to over 1,000 people, mainly from broken glass. It was the largest recorded impact since the Tunguska event in 1908 and highlighted the importance of monitoring near-Earth objects.

Asteroid: (2022 HX1)

Asteroid 2022 HX1 is a near-Earth object (NEO) classified as an Apollo-type, meaning its orbit crosses that of our planet. It has an estimated size between 12 and 26 meters, comparable to a school bus. Its closest approach occurred on April 30, 2022, when it passed approximately 2.49 million kilometers from Earth, posing no impact risk. It orbits the Sun every 665 days (1.82 years), with a minimum distance of 0.84 AU and a maximum of 2.15 AU. Although its trajectory brings it close to Earth, it is not considered potentially hazardous according to NASA simulations.

Hoba (Namibia, discovered in 1920)

The Hoba meteorite, discovered in Namibia in 1920, is the largest intact meteorite ever found on Earth. It weighs approximately 60 tons and is composed primarily of iron and nickel. It is estimated to have fallen around 80,000 years ago, but interestingly, it did not leave a crater, likely due to its low impact velocity. It currently remains at its discovery site and serves as a tourist attraction.

Chicxulub (Mexico, 66 million years ago)

The Chicxulub crater, located on the YucatÃ¡n Peninsula, Mexico, was formed approximately 66 million years ago by the impact of an asteroid about 10 km in diameter. This event caused a mass extinction, including the disappearance of the dinosaurs, by triggering global fires, tsunamis, and a prolonged climatic winter. It is one of the most catastrophic impacts in Earth's history.

Asteroid: (2017 XO2)

Asteroid 2017 XO2 is an Apollo-type near-Earth object, discovered in December 2017. It has an estimated diameter of about 110 meters and an orbit that brings it periodically close to our planet. Although it was initially considered a potential impact risk, this was ruled out after more precise observations. Its next significant approach will occur in 2057, posing no danger.

Barringer Crater (Arizona, USA, ~50,000 years ago)

The Barringer Crater, also known as Meteor Crater, was formed approximately 50,000 years ago in Arizona, USA, by the impact of an iron meteorite about 50 meters in diameter. The crater is 1.2 km wide and 170 meters deep, making it one of the best-preserved in the world. It is clear evidence of the destructive power of cosmic impacts.
